## 1. CAP: no evidence products treat hair shedding

**What happened.** On 9 July 2026 CAP published guidance on advertising hair loss products and services. It recognises three types of hair loss and states of chronic telogen effluvium (hair shedding): "Importantly, neither the ASA nor CAP have seen evidence that products can treat this condition." It adds that "General claims about improving hair growth or thickness are often not permitted unless explicitly supported", that health claims may only be made if authorised on the Great Britain nutrition and health claims (NHC) register, and that "Testimonials do not replace clinical evidence". It lists the evidence failures it repeatedly sees: reliance on study abstracts rather than full studies, studies not related to the product formulation, small unrepresentative sample sizes, lack of control groups or objective measurement, and overreliance on subjective consumer surveys.

**Receipts.**
- CAP News, "Keep your ads a cut above the rest: Advertising products and services for hair loss", 9 July 2026 — **Grade A** — https://www.asa.org.uk/news/keep-your-ads-a-cut-above-the-rest-advertising-products-and-services-for-hair-loss.html
- GB nutrition and health claims register (referenced in guidance) — **Grade A** — https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/great-britain-nutrition-and-health-claims-nhc-register

**Angles.**
- Read the regulator's five evidence failures against the "clinically proven" badges on hair supplements currently sold in the UK.
- Explain the three types of hair loss and which claims are permitted for each — a reusable buyer check.
- The testimonial line: why a wall of before-and-afters is not evidence, in the regulator's own words.

**App connection.** Check evidence fit — paste a hair supplement's claim and the app checks whether it is an authorised NHC claim and whether the evidence matches the type of hair loss being claimed for.

**Why now.** Published 9 July; it is the regulator pre-emptively marking the category's homework before enforcement, which gives us a fair, non-accusatory hook.

## 2. ASA upholds against Kind Patches "GLP-1 Patch"

**What happened.** On 15 July 2026 the ASA upheld two issues against Kind Patches Ltd over a paid-for Facebook ad seen in March 2026 for a product called the "GLP-1 Patch". The caption read "Take control of your cravings and metabolism with the GLP-1 Patch - your smarter path to weight management. Reduce hunger naturally Stay fuller for longer [...]" and on-screen text read "POV: your [sic] a breastfeeding mom who wants to be snatched but can't use those sharp things once a week". Kind Patches told the ASA the patches "did not contain semaglutide, tirzepatide, liraglutide, exenatide or any other active medicinal substance" and that "GLP-1" referred to the body's natural pathway. The ASA ruled the product was medicinal by presentation without the necessary authorisation (rules 12.1, 12.11), and that the ad was irresponsible and included a harmful gender stereotype (rules 1.3, 4.9). The company said the product had been withdrawn from sale and that the post was created by a third-party affiliate, while accepting it remained responsible under the CAP Code. Number of complaints: 1. This is the company's second upheld ruling — an earlier one was published on 4 February 2026.

**Receipts.**
- ASA ruling on Kind Patches Ltd, ref A26-1333913, 15 July 2026 — **Grade A** — https://www.asa.org.uk/rulings/kind-patches-ltd-a26-1333913-kind-patches-ltd.html
- Earlier ASA ruling on Kind Patches Ltd, 4 February 2026 — **Grade A** — https://www.asa.org.uk/rulings/kind-patches-ltd-a25-1310978-kind-patches-ltd.html
- CAP AdviceOnline, Weight control: Food and Food Supplements — **Grade A** — https://www.asa.org.uk/advice-online/weight-control-food-and-food-supplements.html

**Angles.**
- Naming as mechanism: what "GLP-1" on a patch label actually tells you about what's inside — by the company's own account, none of the drugs.
- The affiliate defence: who is responsible for a claim when a third party writes it. The Code's answer is the brand.
- Two upheld rulings in five months on the same company — a compliance-record explainer, sourced only to the public register.

**App connection.** Compare claim vs proof — paste the product name or claim and the app checks whether the mechanism implied by the name is supported, and whether the claim is one a supplement is permitted to make.

**Why now.** Ruled 15 July, today. GLP-1 branding is spreading across non-prescription products and this is the clearest regulator statement yet on borrowing the name.

## 3. Elysium Basis: 50%+ menopause symptom cut in seven days

**What happened.** On 10 July 2026 Nutraceutical Business Review reported Elysium Health's study of its NAD+ product Basis, published in *Frontiers in Aging*. The open-label pilot included 40 healthy women aged 35 and above — 32 self-reporting symptoms associated with the menopausal transition, and eight with no or minimal symptoms. After seven days of supplementation, participants reported reductions of more than 50% in hot flushes, bloating and poor sleep, alongside a significant increase in the ratio of estradiol (E2) to estrone (E1), and the characterisation of a previously unreported NAD+ metabolite. Co-author Dr Marie Migaud is an Elysium research collaborator and said "This study stemmed from my own experience with Basis helping with menopausal symptoms." Co-author Dr Ryan Dellinger is Elysium's VP of Scientific Affairs. The authors called the results "promising" and said they lay groundwork for larger studies; Elysium said it intends to conduct a larger randomised, placebo-controlled trial.

**Receipts.**
- Nutraceutical Business Review, 10 July 2026 — **Grade B** — https://nutraceuticalbusinessreview.com/elysium-health-basis-reduced-menopause-symptoms-oestrogen
- *Frontiers in Aging*, doi 10.3389/fragi.2026.1773667 — **Grade A** — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/aging/articles/10.3389/fragi.2026.1773667/full *(linked from the NBR article; figures above are as reported by NBR, not read from the paper — verify before publishing any number)*

**Angles.**
- What "open-label" means when the outcome is a self-reported symptom: no placebo arm, and menopause symptoms are strongly placebo-responsive.
- The seven-day window: why a one-week readout on hot flushes is a design question, not a result.
- The company's own concession — a larger placebo-controlled trial is planned — read as the honest caption to the headline.

**App connection.** Check evidence fit — paste the claim and the app checks study design against the strength of the claim: is there a control arm, how many people, how long.

## 4. A third of women 35+ are unsure whether they're perimenopausal — and no biomarker exists

**What happened.** A mixed-methods study of more than 7,600 US women aged 35 and older, published 15 July 2026 in *Menopause*, found 34% reported being unsure of their reproductive stage. Uncertainty peaked at 42% among women aged 40–44 and at 37% among those with severe symptom burden. Free-text responses attributed the uncertainty to symptom confusion and attribution (56%), knowledge gaps and information-seeking (28%), and barriers to confirmation and care (16%). The paper notes there is no laboratory test or biomarker to definitively determine perimenopause stage, and that "there has been a propagation of misinformation about menopause and symptom management in recent years".

## 8. The Akkermansia RCT is real; the GLP-1 extension is not

**What happened.** On 9 July 2026 NutraIngredients reported a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial published in *Nature Medicine*. Ninety adults with overweight or obesity completed an eight-week low-energy diet and achieved at least 8% weight loss before entering a 24-week maintenance phase; 80 participants completed. Those taking pasteurized *Akkermansia muciniphila* MucT regained an average of 1.2 kg versus 3.2 kg on placebo, with better preservation of insulin sensitivity (Matsuda Index) and no treatment-related serious adverse events. Dr Anneleen Segers of The Akkermansia Company — which owns the ingredient — said it "could be beneficial… after GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy", while the article states studies evaluating that application have yet to be conducted. On gut–brain and IBS claims, Segers says "these are very preliminary data."

**Receipts.**
- NutraIngredients, 9 July 2026 — **Grade B** — https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2026/07/09/pasteurized-akkermansia-muciniphila-may-help-maintain-weight-loss-after-dieting/
- *Nature Medicine* 32, 2107–2116 (2026), doi 10.1038/s41591-026-04394-7 — **Grade A** *(cited in the NutraIngredients article; not independently fetched)*

**Angles.**
- A rare good-news evidence story: this is what a properly controlled supplement trial looks like, and it earns its result. Use it as the positive benchmark against which weaker weeks are judged.
- The extension gap: the primary endpoint is evidenced, the GLP-1 aftercare hook is not — and the article says so plainly.
- Weight regain after coming off a GLP-1 is a live buyer moment for this audience; what does the evidence actually cover?

**App connection.** Compare claim vs proof — paste the product and the app separates the claim the trial supports (weight-regain maintenance) from the claim it does not (post-GLP-1 use).

**Why now.** Published 9 July, peer-reviewed in a top journal, and the ingredient is already sold to UK consumers.

## 11. CAP: a brand name can itself be an illegal medicinal claim

**What happened.** CAP published "Wishful drinking - hangover cures and the Code" on 9 July 2026, restating that per MHRA advice hangovers and their symptoms are adverse medical conditions, so any food, drink or supplement claiming to treat them makes a prohibited medicinal claim. It confirms indirect wording, hashtags and brand names count as claims, citing prior rulings against Hangcure Ltd, Wild Drinks Group Ltd (Whisp) and Bounce Back Drinks Ltd — the ASA having ruled "Hangcure" was shorthand for "hangover cure" and therefore a claim in its own right.

**Receipts.**
- CAP News, 9 July 2026 — **Grade A** — https://www.asa.org.uk/news/wishful-drinking-hangover-cures-and-the-code.html

**Angles.** The naming principle generalises: when the product name is the claim. Pairs directly with Kind Patches' "GLP-1 Patch" (#2) — same principle, two categories, one week.

**App connection.** Check claim — paste a product name and the app flags where the name itself carries a claim the product isn't authorised to make.

**Why now.** 9 July, and it is the cleanest available explainer of a principle that does real work in the supplement aisle. It is a restatement of existing rules, not new law.

## 17. EHPM summit: EU maximum levels, PA limits, influencer guidance

**What happened.** NutraIngredients reported on 9 July 2026 from EHPM's inaugural Food Supplement Summit (Brussels, 23 June). Public consultation on EU-wide Maximum Permitted Levels for vitamins and minerals opens Q3 2026, with draft regulation expected Q1 2028. EHPM is building an impact assessment covering 100 companies from 20 Member States and five non-EU countries. Pyrrolizidine alkaloid maximum levels for herbal supplements are currently 400 ppb, with discussions to raise to 700 μg/kg (lemon balm, chamomile, peppermint, nettle and others) and 1,000 μg/kg (parsley, sage, St John's wort, raspberry leaves, California poppy, cardoon/artichoke). Proposed MOAH maxima are 10 mg/kg from 2027, tightening to 5 mg/kg in 2030. DG SANTE's Dr Ivona Babic said 197 consultation conclusions have been issued since 2018 — 51% classifying products as novel, 41% non-novel, 8% novel in foods but not in food supplements — and that authorisation "typically takes at least 18 months". EHPM's influencer-marketing guidelines warn influencers not to "guarantee the efficacy of a supplement".

**Receipts.**
- NutraIngredients, 9 July 2026 — **Grade B** — https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2026/07/09/all-change-regulatory-updates-from-the-ehpms-food-supplement-summit/

**Angles.** An industry body telling its own influencers not to guarantee efficacy — worth noting plainly. PA limits govern the herbals sold for menopause and sleep.

**App connection.** Weak. Check dose, indirectly — maximum permitted levels decide which doses stay legal.

**Why now.** 9 July. EU rather than UK; frame for UK relevance carefully.
