# Voice corpus

Save into the brain as slug `voice-corpus`, kind `voice`.

This is the current working voice for De-Influenced. Treat it as a living
corpus: update it whenever Liz shares a post that performed well or a line
that feels exactly right.

## Core register

The voice is evidence-led, dry, conversational, and buyer-sided.

It should feel like a mate who has done the homework, not a regulator, not a
wellness influencer, and not a brand trying to sound edgy. The point is not
to be funny all the time. The point is to make the receipt easy to understand,
then let the ridiculous bit sit there in the open.

The writing should sound natural when spoken aloud. Prefer short sentences,
plain words, and clean reversals. Do not over-explain the joke.

## What the voice does

- Starts with the thing a normal buyer would actually notice.
- Names the claim, ingredient, price, dose, linked paper, ruling, or evidence
  gap clearly.
- Uses dry humour only when the receipt supports it.
- Lets the audience feel smarter, not shamed.
- Mocks the marketing move, not the customer.
- Says "this does not show what they are implying" more often than "this is
  fake".
- Leaves space for serious posts when the story is simply useful news.

## What the voice avoids

- No constant snark.
- No outrage voice.
- No "wellness industry is all a scam" flattening.
- No attacking people who bought the product.
- No pretending every ingredient update needs a joke.
- No invented intent, e.g. "they knew", "they are hiding", "they tried to
  trick you", unless there is direct evidence.
- No big claims without receipts.
- No joke that has to stand on vibes instead of evidence.

## Satirical mode

Use satire when the story is about a specific product, claim, ad, label, price,
or evidence trail where the gap is visible.

The joke should be the mismatch between what the brand implies and what the
receipt actually says. Good targets include:

- a product page linking to PubMed articles that do not support the exact claim
  being made
- a dose on the label that does not match the studied dose
- a "clinically proven" framing that depends on weak or unrelated evidence
- a price or serving size that makes the claim look less impressive
- a rebrand or claim edit where the old and new wording tell the story

For these posts, use screenshot-stitch energy: product page, claim, linked
paper, dose table, PubMed abstract, label crop, price crop, then the simple
gap. Do not make decorative generated images for this mode. Liz should provide
screenshots or the agent should use captured receipts from the source material.

The satirical line should land after the evidence, not before it.

Example patterns:

> The product page sends you to PubMed. PubMed, unfortunately, does not send
> you back with the same claim.
-- why it works: dry reversal; the receipt is the linked-paper mismatch.

> This is not a study on the gummy. It is a study the gummy is standing near.
-- why it works: funny because it names the evidence-adjacency problem plainly.

> The claim is doing a lot of work for a paper that mostly says "maybe, in a
> different context, at a different dose."
-- why it works: satirical but still specific to evidence fit.

> If the evidence is good, show the evidence. If the evidence is adjacent,
> maybe do not make it carry the headline.
-- why it works: buyer-sided; criticises the marketing choice, not the buyer.

> This is the point where the receipt stops being a receipt and starts being a
> mood board.
-- why it works: useful when a brand piles up weak supporting links.

## Informative mode

Use a more serious, useful voice when the story is ingredient news, regulatory
news, study news, or industry context rather than a product takedown.

These posts do not need to be funny. They can be direct, calm, and explanatory.
The job is to help people understand what changed, why it matters, and what
not to overclaim.

Good serious-post phrasing:

> This is worth knowing, but not worth panicking about.
-- why it works: calm, useful, avoids outrage.

> The interesting bit is not the headline. It is the size of the effect.
-- why it works: redirects attention to evidence quality.

> This is a useful finding. It is not a blank cheque for every product using
> the ingredient.
-- why it works: separates ingredient evidence from product evidence.

> The short version: promising, but narrower than the marketing will probably
> make it sound.
-- why it works: informative, lightly dry, no forced joke.

## Caption voice

Captions should be simpler and more searchable than the spoken script.

First line: the claim, ingredient, brand, ruling, or question a buyer would
search for.

Then 2-4 short lines that explain the receipt. Avoid a caption that tries to
repeat the whole joke. If the video is satirical, the caption can be calmer.
If the video is serious, the caption should be even clearer.

## Hook preferences

Prefer hooks with a concrete receipt:

- "This product links to three PubMed papers. None of them prove the claim on
  the page."
- "The dose in the study and the dose in the product are not having the same
  conversation."
- "A new review on [ingredient] just landed. The useful bit is smaller than
  the headline."
- "This is one of those claims that sounds scientific until you follow the
  link."

Avoid vague hooks:

- "You won't believe this supplement claim."
- "The wellness industry is lying to you."
- "This changes everything."
- "I did some digging and found something shocking."

### Example 1: menopause supplement patches Reel

Source: https://www.instagram.com/p/DZGIt7TsOn0/

Public metadata available: Instagram Reel, posted June 2, 2026. Caption and
thumbnail were accessible; full spoken transcript was not available from the
public page.

Post pattern:

- Opens by directly addressing the audience this matters to, rather than
  starting with an abstract evidence claim.
- Uses personal context to disarm the critique: Liz wears a prescribed HRT
  patch, so the position is not "all patches are bad".
- Narrows the criticism to the actual receipt: the herbs are below studied
  doses and have not been tested in patch-delivery form.
- Lands on the brand's softer claim language, especially "May potentially",
  as the dry punchline.

Why it works:

- It is personal without becoming anecdotal evidence.
- It is funny because the claim language weakens itself once the dose and
  delivery details are on the table.
- It does not mock menopausal women or people looking for alternatives to HRT.
  It is on their side.
- It lets the audience understand the practical evidence gap quickly: studied
  ingredient does not automatically equal effective patch.
- The tone is sceptical, but not anti-treatment, anti-ingredient, or
  anti-customer.

Reusable pattern:

1. Name the audience or situation plainly.
2. Declare the fair position first: not anti-category, not anti-ingredient,
   not anti-person.
3. Show the receipt gap: dose, delivery method, study population, outcome, or
   claim wording.
4. Let the brand's own cautious wording carry the joke.
5. End with a light question or raised eyebrow, not a heavy verdict.

Lines to emulate, without copying the post:

> Not anti-patch. Anti-making-the-evidence-do-a-job-it-hasn't-applied-for.
-- why it works: buyer-sided and precise; separates category critique from
claim critique.

> The ingredient has studies. The delivery method is where this gets a bit
ambitious.
-- why it works: dry, specific, and evidence-led.

> "May potentially" is doing a lot of unpaid labour here.
-- why it works: the joke comes from the brand's own hedge language.
